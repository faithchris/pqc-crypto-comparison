Post-Quantum vs Classical Cryptography

A hands-on comparison of elliptic-curve schemes (ECDSA, ECDH) with NIST post-quantum standards (ML-DSA-44, ML-KEM-768) — including a live web demo, reproducible benchmarks, and analysis of the security–performance tradeoffs facing real HTTPS deployments.

Abstract

We provide a small web application that exercises ECDSA (P-256) and ML-DSA-44 for signing and verification, and ECDH (P-256) and ML-KEM-768 for key agreement. For ML-KEM we emphasize the ciphertext produced by encapsulation (base64-encoded in the browser because ciphertext is binary); the shared secret is derived by decapsulation but is not the on-wire encrypted payload in the same sense as ciphertext. ECDH establishes a shared secret without a ciphertext comparable to ML-KEM's.

Keywords: ML-KEM; ECDH; key encapsulation; Shor's algorithm; HTTPS; Merkle tree certificates

Background: Why Post-Quantum Cryptography?

Shor's Algorithm and Classical Risk

Shor's algorithm is a quantum algorithm that solves the integer factorization and discrete logarithm problems in polynomial time on a sufficiently large, fault-tolerant quantum computer. RSA relies on the hardness of factoring large composites; elliptic-curve schemes used in practice (including ECDSA and ECDH over P-256) rely on variants of the discrete-logarithm problem. If Shor's algorithm could be run at cryptographically relevant scales, those problems would become easy, breaking confidentiality and authenticity for systems that depend only on those assumptions.

How PQC Differs

Post-quantum schemes are chosen so that, as of current knowledge, no polynomial-time quantum attack analogous to Shor's is known. Lattice-based standards such as ML-DSA and ML-KEM rest on different hardness assumptions (structured lattice problems) for which Shor's algorithm does not directly apply in the same way.

Classical

ECDSA & ECDH (P-256)

Elliptic-curve math. Smaller keys and signatures. Vulnerable to future quantum attacks via Shor's algorithm.

Post-Quantum

ML-DSA-44 & ML-KEM-768

Lattice-based (Module-LWE). Resistant to known quantum attacks. Larger artifacts and higher CPU cost on classical hardware.

Scope note: ML-DSA and ML-KEM target public-key (asymmetric) problems — signatures and key encapsulation. Symmetric cryptography (e.g., AES-GCM) is generally less threatened by Shor's algorithm; real deployments typically hybridize PQC asymmetric primitives with efficient symmetric encryption.

Methods & Toolchain

Two complementary tools produce the results on this page:

  • Web demo (encryptionCompare_v1.py) — a Flask app at http://127.0.0.1:5000 that runs signature and key-agreement comparisons in the browser, reporting timings (ms) and artifact sizes (bytes/bits). Fresh key material is generated per request.
  • Offline benchmarks (benchmark.py) — repeats measurements over many runs, exports CSV tables, and generates matplotlib figures saved under results/.

Measurement conventions

  • Signature sizes — length of the digital signature object (not the user's message).
  • ML-KEM ciphertext — the principal on-wire output of encapsulation; decapsulation recovers the shared secret.
  • ECDH — no KEM-style ciphertext; peers exchange public keys and derive a shared secret.

Benchmark environment: Linux 6.8.0 · Python 3.12 · N = 30 runs for aggregate timings · libraries: cryptography, dilithium-py, kyber-py

Key Findings

  • Size tradeoff is dramatic. ML-DSA signatures are ~34× larger than ECDSA (2,420 B vs 72 B). ML-KEM ciphertexts (~1,088 B) have no direct ECDH analogue — ECDH exchanges only public keys (~91 B).
  • Signing is the biggest timing gap. ML-DSA sign averaged ~37 ms vs ~0.19 ms for ECDSA — roughly 195× slower in our environment. Verification and key-agreement gaps are smaller but still measurable.
  • Results match expected PQC costs. Larger lattice parameters buy quantum resistance at the price of bandwidth and CPU — the same tensions engineers face when quantum-proofing TLS and certificate chains.
  • Measurement protocol matters. Key-generation time should be reported separately from steady-state encapsulation/sign loops when evaluating production-like workloads.
  • Educational scope. The Flask demo uses a development server and fresh keys per request — suitable for teaching and experimentation, not production deployment.

Real-World Deployment & HTTPS

Deploying PQC on the web exposes practical tensions between security and performance. Industry reporting notes that quantum-resistant cryptographic material in certificate transparency can be on the order of ~40× larger than classical material, while a typical X.509 chain today is already ~4 KB during TLS handshake setup.

Larger handshakes slow TLS, risk compatibility with middleboxes, and may push users to disable new protections. The ecosystem is exploring Merkle tree certificates (MTCs) — structures that verify inclusion in a large set using a compact proof instead of transmitting a long serialized chain of signatures.

Google, Cloudflare, and certificate authorities are actively testing ML-DSA and related material so forgery would require breaking both classical and post-quantum assumptions. Our classroom metrics — larger artifacts, higher CPU cost — mirror the constraints engineers face when quantum-proofing HTTPS.
